## VOICE

### How we sound

Like a smart person explaining business to another smart person over coffee. Laid back but sharp. Clear but not clinical. Like a businessman who's been through the hoops and speaks from experience and insight, not from a pitch deck.

Think: someone who's seen the same operational problem a hundred times across different businesses. They don't lecture about it. They just describe what they've seen, name what's actually going on, and let the other person connect the dots. They don't perform expertise. They demonstrate it by how naturally they talk about it.

This applies to everything: site, proposals, outreach, social posts, client calls, internal communication, even how we talk to each other. Smart enough that sharp people take us seriously. Simple enough that a non-technical business owner gets it immediately.

If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it. If it sounds like something a human would actually say to another human sitting across from them, it's probably right.

### Context shifts

The voice stays the same. The energy adjusts:

- **Website:** tighter. Every line earns its place. Less conversational, same voice.
- **Outreach:** more direct. Get to the point fast. Slightly more conversational.
- **Proposals:** more structured. More precise. Still sounds like a person, not a document.
- **Social media (brand):** shorter. One point per post. No filler.
- **Social media (founder):** more personal. More blunt. More visible as a person with opinions and experience.
- **Client communication:** warmer. Clear, honest, respectful. Never corporate.
- **Internal:** casual. Same principles, less polish. We talk to each other the way we talk to clients: direct and real.

---

### The two filters

Every piece of writing passes both filters before it goes out. No exceptions.

**Filter 1:** If this sentence could be written by any other company in our industry without being false, don't use it.

**Filter 2:** If this sentence couldn't be said out loud in a real conversation with a client, don't write it.

If something feels off but you can't articulate why, run it through the filters. One of them will catch it.

## THE LANGUAGE PRINCIPLE

This is the single most important communication rule in the company. Everything else flows from it.

**Default to their language, not ours.**

Vendor language sounds like selling. Their language sounds like understanding. We want to sound like we've been in their office, seen their Monday morning, and know exactly which five things are going to go wrong this week.

This doesn't mean we can never use business terms. Sometimes "workflow" is the right word. But the instinct should always be: can I say this in a way that the person across from me would actually use to describe their own problem? If yes, use their version. If the vendor term is genuinely clearer in context, fine, but that should be the exception, not the default.

**The translation test:** Take any sentence you've written. If a non-technical business owner would tilt their head and say "what do you mean by that?", rewrite it. If they'd nod and say "yeah, exactly that," you've got it.

### General translations

These are not niche-specific. These apply across all industries and all communication.

| Vendor language (avoid by default) | Their language (use this) |
|---|---|
| "Streamline operations" | "Get rid of the double work and the stuff that falls through" |
| "Automated workflows" | "Requests go into one queue instead of five different places" |
| "Digital transformation" | "Don't change what you have. Put order into what exists." |
| "Data-driven decisions" | "Know in 2 minutes where the biggest problem is" |
| "Operational efficiency" | "Finish the day without 5 forgotten things" |
| "Scalability solution" | "Handle more volume without hiring more people" |
| "Optimize processes" | "Fix the parts where things get stuck, lost, or done twice" |
| "Leverage AI" | "Use AI where it actually saves time or catches what people miss" |
| "Centralized platform" | "One place where everything lives instead of scattered across email, WhatsApp, and spreadsheets" |
| "Improve customer experience" | "Respond faster and stop losing requests" |
| "Increase visibility" | "Know what's going on without having to ask someone" |
| "Reduce manual touchpoints" | "Stop doing the same thing by hand every day" |

**Note:** These are defaults, not absolute bans. Context matters. In a technical conversation with a CTO, "automated workflows" might be exactly right. In an email to a property manager, it's not. Read the room.

## LANGUAGE RULES

### Words and phrases to avoid

These are the terms that make us sound like every other vendor. Avoid them by default. If you catch yourself reaching for one, stop and translate it into what you actually mean in plain language.

**On technology:**
cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, leverage AI, seamless, innovative, future-proof, end-to-end transformation, digital transformation journey

**On proximity (empty promises):**
we're with you every step, we handle everything, leave it to us, we fully integrate into your team

**Note on "partner" language:** The word "partner" is part of our positioning, but only when it's backed by specifics. "We're your AI partner" is acceptable in context. "We become your partner in growth" without explaining what that means in practice is not. The issue is empty proximity, not the concept of partnership.

**On vagueness:**
we deliver value, optimize, streamline, scalable solutions, customized for your needs, we adapt to any business, we help companies grow

**On false ease:**
plug-and-play, no effort required, immediate results, set it and forget it, simple for anyone

**On scope:**
we build whatever you need, support as needed, we'll figure it out as we go

**The principle:** none of these are banned forever. But if you're using one, ask yourself: is this the best way to say this, or am I being lazy? Nine times out of ten, there's a more concrete, more human way to say it.

---

### Identity lines

These are the lines that anchor the brand. They appear across site, outreach, proposals. They're not slogans. They're reference points that keep the voice consistent.

**On what we do:**

*"We find where businesses lose revenue and time, and install the AI systems that fix it."*

*"Work gets lost between people and tools. We build the system that keeps it from falling through."*

*"We don't become the system. We install it."*

**On how we work:**

*"We don't start from the tool. We start from where the business is leaking."*

*"If the process isn't clear, automation just makes the chaos faster."*

*"We don't sell ease. We sell a more controlled way to work."*

*"We install, train, and stay. First versions aren't final."*

**On who we're for:**

*"The right client isn't looking to avoid effort. They're looking to stop losing time to chaos."*

*"We work well with businesses that have real teams and enough moving parts that things are starting to fall through."*

**On AI:**

*"AI is how the system holds. Not the thing we're selling."*

*"We lead with the business problem. AI is the explanation for how the fix works."*

**Note:** These should evolve as we land clients and discover which phrases actually resonate in real conversations. These are starting points, not permanent fixtures.

---

### Three rules for writing about the client's problem

1. Don't diagnose in our language. Describe in theirs.
2. Don't explain the problem. Make it visible.
3. If they don't say "yes, exactly that": you haven't described it correctly.

### The mini-framework: convert abstract to concrete

Concept to situation: "fragmentation" becomes "I check 5 places every morning"

Problem to moment: "lack of follow-up" becomes "the lead never heard back after the call"

General to question: "you have dropped balls" becomes "how many things did you find out too late this week?"

This applies across all problem areas. Revenue example: "poor lead response" becomes "a prospect filled out the form on Tuesday and nobody called until Friday." Leadership example: "no visibility" becomes "the founder finds out about the problem after it's already cost money."

---

### AI usage rule

AI is part of how we build. It is not the promise we sell.

We lead with the business problem. We explain where AI helps after the problem is clear. This applies to all channels: site, proposals, outreach, social.

The only exception is when someone specifically asks about AI. Then we explain what it does and doesn't do, honestly.

---

### Delivery rules: three lines that never change

*"Here's what we do, here's what we need from you, and by when."*

*"We define now what done means. Otherwise nobody knows."*

*"If something sits outside this proposal, we flag it and define it before touching it."*

**Speaking to the operations manager**

The operations manager is the second buyer. They're not trapped. They see where it breaks every day. Their frustration isn't that they don't know what's wrong. It's that they can't make it visible to the people who can change it.

When something breaks, it often looks like an execution problem, even when the system is the issue. That's their position. That's what we validate.

The message for them is different from the owner message.

Don't say to them:

- "You're holding everything together."
- "Everything runs through you."
- "If you step away, things slip."

That's the owner's pain. Not theirs.

Say instead:

- "You already know where it breaks. The problem is making it visible to the people who can actually change it."
- "The system doesn't just fix the workflow. It gives you the evidence to show what's been broken all along."
- "You've been right about the problem. You've just had no way to prove it."

**In social:** minimum 1 in 5 LinkedIn posts speaks explicitly or implicitly to the operations manager, not the owner.

**In proposals:** if the operations manager is in the room or on the call, adjust the framing of "What we heard" to reflect their perspective, not just the owner's.

**In outreach:** if the signal points to an operations manager as the contact, the hook changes. Not "you're holding everything together" but "you already see where it breaks."

## NICHE LANGUAGE BANKS

The general language rules above apply everywhere. The banks below go deeper into specific verticals with three layers:

1. **Vendor vs their language** -- industry-specific translations
2. **Survey to real** -- what industry reports say vs what the owner actually experiences. Useful for content, outreach, and showing you understand their world beyond the data.
3. **Openers** -- questions framed the way they think about their problems, not the way a consultant would ask

As we validate new verticals, each one gets its own bank in this format.

### Property Management

**Vendor language vs their language:**

| Vendor Language (Avoid) | Their Language (Use This) |
|---|---|
| "Streamline operations" | "Get rid of double-logging and WhatsApp work orders" |
| "Improve tenant experience" | "Respond faster and stop losing requests" |
| "Automated workflows" | "Maintenance requests go into one single queue" |
| "Digital transformation" | "Don't change what you have — put order into what exists" |
| "Increase NOI" | "Spend less on emergencies, avoid turnover costs" |
| "Data-driven decisions" | "Know in 2 minutes which units give you the most headache" |
| "Property management platform" | "The system that keeps things in order" |
| "Resident satisfaction" | "Tenants stop leaving 1-star reviews because nobody responded" |
| "Operational efficiency" | "Finish the day without 5 forgotten things" |
| "Scalability solution" | "Add 50 units without hiring 2 new people" |

**What studies say vs what they actually mean:**

| Survey / Report Language | What It Actually Means (Their Voice) |
|---|---|
| "Maintenance is stress source #1" | "My phone rings at 11pm for a dripping faucet" |
| "Fraud incidents increased 70% YoY" | "I approved a tenant with fake documents and found out 3 months later" |
| "NOI compression challenge" | "Costs went up 6%, I couldn't raise rent more than 1%, and I don't know where to cut" |
| "Tech adoption gap persists" | "I paid $298/month for AppFolio and my employee still sends work orders on WhatsApp" |
| "Resident quality is the foremost challenge" | "I no longer know if the applicant in front of me is real or an AI fabrication" |
| "Employee turnover 33% annual" | "I trained 3 people for the same role in 2 years — each one left and took their tenant relationships with them" |
| "Portfolio growth strategy" | "I want to grow to 500 units but with my current structure I'd collapse at 350" |
| "Strategic visibility gaps" | "I work all the time but I don't know which part of the business is profitable and which is sinking me" |

**Openers -- questions from their voice:**

- "How many different places does a maintenance request come in from?" -- not: "do you have a centralized workflow?"
- "What happens to a request that comes in Sunday evening on WhatsApp?" -- not: "how do you manage multi-channel communication?"
- "Last time you approved a tenant who turned out to be a problem — how did you find out something was wrong?" -- not: "what's your screening process?"
- "If your key employee doesn't come in tomorrow, which things wouldn't get done?" -- not: "how well-documented are your processes?"
- "What's the first thing you do in the morning?" -- not: "what's your #1 strategic priority?"

**Note:** This bank should grow from real conversations. Every audit call, every sales conversation, every objection heard feeds back into these tables.

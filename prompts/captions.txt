Cosmo's voice is blunt, practical, and personal. He talks like someone who's been through the hoops and doesn't have patience for fluff. He names uncomfortable things. He says "I noticed" and "here's what surprised me" — not "businesses should consider" or "it's important to leverage."

LANGUAGE RULES

Use THEIR language — the SMB owner's, the ops manager's. Not vendor language.

state-of-the-art, innovative, future-proof, digital transformation, plug-and-play,

HOOK-FIRST PRINCIPLE

Every caption starts with a hook. The hook is the first thing — before any context, before any setup. If the first line doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters.

Good hook patterns (pick the one that fits the idea):
- Contrarian: "Nobody talks about the part where this breaks."
- Authority/observation: "I've looked at a dozen service businesses this year. Same problem every time."
- Curiosity gap: "The fix took 20 minutes. Finding the problem took 3 months."
- Direct address: "If you're still doing [specific task] by hand, watch this."
- Result-first: "Cut 4 hours a week from our intake process. Here's exactly how."

---

PLATFORM RULES

TikTok:
- Hook must land in the first 2 seconds of reading — punchy, scroll-stopping, no warm-up
- Max 150 characters for the caption body (hook + CTA)
- Hashtags: 3-5 max, kept tight (#SmallBusiness #AITools #Automation or relevant niche tags)
- CTA: follow for more, comment your answer, or tell me if this happens to you
- Tone: most casual, fastest — like texting someone who gets it
- No corporate framing. Ever.

Instagram:
- Hook + value proposition in the first line (before the "more" fold)
- Use line breaks to create visual rhythm — one thought per line
- Can expand on the idea: up to 2200 characters, but earn every line
- Hashtags: 5-10 at the end, not woven in
- CTA: save this, tag someone who needs this, or a question that invites a comment
- Slightly more room to breathe than TikTok, but still punchy

YouTube Shorts:
- Title: searchable + curiosity gap, under 80 characters — think "what would someone search to find this?"
- Description: expand on the value, 2-3 sentences. Can include a CTA to subscribe or check a link.
- No hashtag spam in the description
- Energy: someone discovering something useful, not someone performing content

LinkedIn:
- Professional angle of the same content — same idea, shifted framing
- Lead with an observation or a specific situation, not a hot take
- Longer form is fine here (200-400 words) — LinkedIn rewards depth
- First-person, opinionated, backed by something real ("I've seen this in three businesses in the last 6 months")
- No hashtag spam — 2-3 relevant tags max, appended at the end
- CTA: ask a question, invite disagreement, or point to a resource
- Avoid: bullet-point listicles, "excited to share", vague leadership lessons, anything generic

## PHILOSOPHY

### What social media is for us

Social media is how we get in front of people who don't know us yet, build trust with the ones who do, and eventually turn attention into conversations and calls.

It's not separate from the business. It's the public version of how we think and talk. Same voice, same honesty, same language. If it sounds different from how we'd talk to a client in person, something's off.

### Five principles

1. Social is how we talk to potential clients in public, using the same language we'd use in a real conversation.
2. The best posts come from real work, real conversations, and real observations. Not from brainstorming sessions.
3. Recognition beats explanation. If they don't see themselves in it, they won't reach the insight.
4. Proof beats opinion. Especially at our stage.
5. Consistency beats volume. Posting regularly at whatever pace is sustainable beats going hard for two weeks and disappearing.

### Voice on social

Same voice as everywhere else: a smart person who's seen this problem before, talking about it like they would over coffee. Not performing expertise. Not dumbing it down.

Both filters still apply:

**Filter 1:** Could any competitor post this without it being false? Don't post it.

**Filter 2:** Could this be said out loud in a real conversation? If not, don't write it.

## THE FUNNEL

Social content isn't one thing. It serves different purposes depending on where the viewer is in their awareness of us and their own problem. Understanding this determines what to post and why.

### Top of funnel: get eyeballs

**Goal:** reach. Get as many people as possible to see us, even if most of them aren't our target client.

**What this looks like:** broad AI content, trending topics, newsworthy industry moments, things that are interesting to a wide audience. This content doesn't need to fit neatly into our business pillars. If it's trending, relevant to the AI space, and we have something to say about it, that's enough. The point is to reach people who are mildly interested in AI, get them to notice us, and plant a seed. Some of those people will come back weeks or months later when they have a real problem.

**Why this matters especially early on:** content that's too specific too early gets killed before it reaches terminal velocity. Social platforms reward content that gets engagement fast. Broad, interesting content builds the base of attention that makes the more specific content viable later.

**The reality:** some of this content won't directly relate to what we sell. That's fine. The founder posting about a new AI tool launch, or reacting to industry drama, or explaining a trend in plain language, that's all valid top of funnel. It makes people think "this person knows what they're talking about" and "this person is interesting to follow." That's the job at this stage.

### Middle of funnel: qualify attention

**Goal:** relevance. Start filtering the broad audience toward business owners who have (or might have) the problems we solve.

**What this looks like:** content that speaks to business owners generally, not yet ultra-specific to one vertical or one problem. The content pillars (Mirror, Naming, Pattern Break, etc.) live primarily here. The content is wide enough to catch different types of business owners but specific enough that non-business-owners start to self-select out.

**Examples:** a post about how most businesses lose leads not because of bad marketing but because nobody follows up fast enough. That resonates with a property manager, a clinic owner, a construction company, and a recruiting agency. It's specific enough to be useful but broad enough to reach all of them.

**The shift over time:** as the audience grows and we learn which types of business owners engage most, middle of funnel content can narrow. But in the beginning, staying wider here is strategic.

### Bottom of funnel: convert

**Goal:** conversion. Speak directly to a specific audience about their specific problem, and move them toward a conversation.

**What this looks like:** highly specific content aimed at one vertical or one pain point. "If you manage 200+ rental units and your maintenance requests come in through four different channels, this is what's actually happening in your business." This kind of content gets fewer views but the people who do see it are exactly the people we want to talk to.

**When this makes sense:** once we have a real audience base and enough data to know which verticals and problems resonate most. Bottom of funnel content posted to a small account with no audience gets almost zero reach. Bottom of funnel content posted to an established account with 10K+ followers finds the right 50 people and starts conversations.

**The rule:** don't over-index on bottom of funnel content in the early stages. It feels productive because it's specific, but the math doesn't work without the audience base that top and middle of funnel build first.

### How the funnel shapes the content mix

In the early phase (building audience): heavily weighted toward top and middle of funnel. The majority of posts should be interesting to a broad audience or to business owners generally.

As the audience grows: the mix shifts toward middle and bottom. More specific, more vertical-targeted, more conversion-oriented.

This isn't a rigid formula. It's a lens for deciding what to post on any given day. "Is this going to reach new people, or is this going to convert people who already know us?" Both are needed. The ratio just changes over time.

## SHORT-FORM CONTENT CATEGORIES

For short-form content (TikTok, Reels, Shorts), every piece fits into one of three categories. These are the frames that make someone stop scrolling.

### Pain

You're speaking to a specific pain that a potential client is facing. You describe what their day actually looks like, what's broken, what's costing them money or time. The goal is recognition: "that's exactly what's happening to me."

Pain content is the most reliable driver of engagement because people feel seen. It works at every funnel stage, but it's strongest in the middle of funnel where business owners are recognizing their own problems.

### Prize

You're giving them something they want. A solution, a framework, a free resource, a way of thinking about their problem that makes it feel solvable. Prize content earns trust because you're leading with value before asking for anything.

This includes: quick tips that actually work, free tools or templates, brief overviews of a process (with a CTA to download the full version or book a call for the details), and "here's how I'd fix this" breakdowns.

### News

Something newsworthy from the industry. A new tool launch, a company making a big move, an AI development that matters, drama in the ecosystem, a trend that business owners should know about.

News content is pure top of funnel. It catches people who are broadly interested in AI or business, even if they don't have a specific problem right now. It positions the founder as someone who's plugged in and has perspective, not just someone trying to sell.

### How these relate to the content pillars

The pain/prize/news categories are the *frame* (what makes someone stop scrolling). The content pillars (Mirror, Naming, Pattern Break, etc.) are the *structure* (what the post actually does once they're watching). A single piece of content is both:

- A Mirror post using a Pain frame: "You check five places every morning and still feel like something's slipping."
- A Process post using a Prize frame: "Here's exactly how I'd find where your business is leaking revenue. Three steps."
- A Pattern Break post using a News frame: "Everyone's excited about this new AI tool. Here's why it won't fix the actual problem."

## CONTENT PILLARS

Six pillars. Every post belongs to at least one. If it doesn't fit any, either it's top-of-funnel content (which is fine) or it shouldn't be posted.

---

**Pillar 1. Mirror**

Job: immediate recognition.

You describe exactly what their day looks like. Specific situations, not categories. The reader sees themselves before they finish the sentence. This works across any problem area: an owner drowning in email, a lead that went cold because nobody followed up, a team member doing the same data entry three times.

Rule: don't explain the problem. Show it. No diagnosis, no solution in the same post.

---

**Pillar 2. Naming**

Job: authority. You name something they feel but haven't articulated.

You identify a pattern you've seen across businesses and name it clearly. This could be an operational pattern, a revenue leak pattern, a leadership trap, anything that repeats.

Rule: one single insight per post. Not a list of insights.

---

**Pillar 3. Pattern Break**

Job: shareability. You challenge something everyone assumes.

You take something everyone believes and show why it's wrong or incomplete. "More tools don't fix coordination." "AI won't help if the process is already broken." "Hiring another person won't solve a systems problem."

Rule: your position must be defensible. Not provocation for its own sake.

---

**Pillar 4. Proof**

Job: concrete evidence. You show it works.

Real cases, real results, real moments from implementation. When we have client results, this pillar carries the most weight. Until then, proof of thinking: analysis we've done, patterns we've mapped, research that surprised us.

Rule: a concrete number or specific moment in the first sentence. No introduction.

---

**Pillar 5. Process**

Job: education and trust. You show how you think.

You explain how you'd approach a problem, how you run an audit, how you build a system, how you think through where a business is leaking. This builds trust because it shows a real process, not just a pitch.

Process content can be brief (a 60-second overview) or it can be a lead-in to something longer: "Here's the high-level version. If you want the full breakdown, download the guide / book a call / check the link."

Rule: show the real process. Not the idealized version.

---

**Pillar 6. Response**

Job: answer a recurring question, objection, or confusion publicly.

You take something you hear repeatedly and answer it in a post. "We already have a CRM." "We tried AI and it didn't work." "We're too small for this." These are real objections from real conversations.

Rule: the objection or question must be real. Something you've actually heard. Not invented resistance.

## CALLS TO ACTION

Every piece of content should either build trust or move someone toward a next step. Not every post needs a CTA, but when one is included:

**Primary CTAs (what we want people to do):**
- Book a call / start a conversation ("If this sounds like your business, shoot us a message")
- Download a free resource ("We put together a free guide on X, link in bio")
- Engage ("Have you seen this in your business? Tell me in the comments")

**CTA philosophy:**
- In the early stages, the default CTA is conversational: "shoot us a message," "tell me if this resonates," "DM me." We're building relationships, not running a funnel yet.
- Free resources (guides, templates, checklists) are a way to capture interest from people who aren't ready for a call. We should always have at least one freebie available to link to.
- Hard CTAs ("book your free audit now") should be used sparingly. Maximum 1 in 5 posts. The rest build credibility. People reach out because of what you consistently say, not because of one CTA.
- The CTA should feel natural, not bolted on. If the post doesn't naturally lead to an ask, don't force one.

## CONTENT SOURCING

Content comes from two places: our own work and experience, and reference material we find across the internet. Both are valid. Both feed the content machine.

### From our own work and conversations

- **Call notes:** what did a prospect say that you've heard before? Mirror post.
- **Audit observations:** what did you find that surprised even the client? Naming or Proof post.
- **Proposal language:** what problem did you write in "What we heard"? That's a post.
- **Implementation moments:** what happened this week that shows the system working or breaking? Proof or Process post.
- **Objections:** what did someone push back on in a call or DM? Response post.
- **Real screenshots or workflows:** anonymized, with mechanism explained. Process or Proof post.
- **Conversations with other business owners:** what came up over dinner, at an event, in a casual chat? If it relates to AI or business, it's a post.
- **Research and analysis:** what pattern did you spot while studying a vertical? Naming or Pattern Break post.
- **Industry news:** what's happening in AI, in business tech, in a specific vertical? News frame content.

### From reference material (other people's content)

This is a major content source. We're constantly scanning social media, newsletters, podcasts, and other creators for content that we can spin into our own perspective.

**How it works:** find a piece of content that resonates (a video, a post, an article, a take). Run it through our lens: what's our angle on this? How does this connect to what we do? What would we add, disagree with, or reframe? Then create our own version with our own voice, our own examples, and our own positioning.

**This is what the Telegram bot content system is for.** The founder finds content pieces across the internet, drops the link into the Telegram bot, and it enters the content creation pipeline. The system extracts the core idea, and from there we spin it into our own content: same topic, our flavor.

**What this is not:** copying someone else's content or restating their points word for word. The reference material is a starting point. The output is original content that reflects how we think about the topic.

**Good sources for reference material:**
- Other AI agencies and consultants (what are they saying? what do we agree or disagree with?)
- Business operations content creators (what patterns are they describing that we also see?)
- Industry-specific content (property management forums, construction ops channels, clinic management discussions)
- AI tool launches and product announcements (news frame opportunities)
- Trending content in the AI space (top of funnel opportunities)

### The weekly planning question

"What happened in real work last week that someone else would recognize? And what did we see online this week that we have a take on?"

## REPURPOSING

- 1 good LinkedIn post becomes 1 TikTok script
- 1 audit observation becomes 1 Mirror post and 1 Naming video
- 1 case study outcome becomes 3 posts: Proof / Mechanism / Before-After
- 1 recurring objection becomes 1 Response post on LinkedIn and 1 direct-to-camera TikTok
- 1 proposal insight becomes 1 Pattern Break post
- 1 interesting conversation becomes a "here's what I was talking about with someone this week" founder post
- 1 longer process breakdown becomes a teaser post + downloadable resource

**The rule:** if you've written it in a proposal, said it in a call, or thought about it in the shower, it probably belongs in a post. You've already done the thinking. The post is just packaging it.

## CONTENT FORMATS & DISTRIBUTION

Content is organized by format, not by platform. The same piece of content gets distributed across every platform that supports that format.

### Short-form video

The primary content format. One video, distributed everywhere.

**Platforms:** TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Facebook Reels

**What it is:** 60-90 second direct-to-camera or screen walkthrough videos. One point per video. Pain, Prize, or News frame. This is where most of the content pillars live.

**Distribution logic:** one video gets posted across all short-form platforms. The video stays the same. Captions may be adapted slightly per platform, but the content is identical. Produce once, distribute everywhere.

### Long-form video

Deeper content for people actively searching for solutions or already following us.

**Platforms:** YouTube (primary), can be excerpted into short-form clips

**What it is:** tutorials, process deep dives, full audit walkthroughs, "how I'd fix this business" breakdowns, longer educational content. This is where Process pillar content can go deep.

**Distribution logic:** the long-form piece lives on YouTube. Clips and highlights get cut into short-form and distributed across TikTok, Reels, and Shorts.

### Image / text-based

Static content for platforms that reward it.

**Platforms:** LinkedIn (text posts), Instagram (carousels), can be repurposed to Facebook

**What it is:** LinkedIn text posts (the primary written format), Instagram carousels (visual breakdowns, before/after, step-by-step), infographics, and any visual content that explains a concept.

**Distribution logic:** a strong LinkedIn text post can become an Instagram carousel with the same points visualized. A carousel can be summarized into a LinkedIn post. They feed each other.

### Platform roles (why each platform matters)

| Platform | Primary job | Content format |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn | Credibility, lead generation, business conversations | Text posts, carousels, occasional video |
| TikTok | Reach, discovery, new eyeballs | Short-form video |
| Instagram | Credibility reinforcement, visual trust | Reels (from TikTok), carousels, stories |
| YouTube | Long-form depth, searchability, evergreen content | Long-form video, Shorts (from TikTok) |
| Facebook | Distribution into groups and communities | Repurposed from LinkedIn and short-form video |

**Hierarchy:** LinkedIn and TikTok are primary. Instagram and YouTube secondary. Facebook is distribution only.

## WHAT WE AVOID POSTING

These aren't absolute bans, but if you find yourself writing any of these, stop and ask why:

- Motivational quotes without a specific business situation attached
- "Excited to announce..." for anything
- Generic thought leadership with nothing real behind it
- Screenshots of metrics without explaining the mechanism that produced them
- Listicles that could have been written by anyone
- Apologies for absence. Just come back and post something good.
- Posts starting with "In today's fast-paced world..." or any variation
- Anything that could be posted by any other company in our space without being false

## KPI LOGIC

**Platform-level signals worth watching:**
- Profile views and follows (are we growing?)
- Saves and shares (is the content valuable enough to keep or pass along?)
- Comments and DM replies (are people engaging?)
- Video retention (are people watching past the hook?)

**The only metrics that actually matter for the business:**
- Audit calls booked that trace back to social content
- Warm replies that mention a specific post
- Recognition in sales calls: "I saw your post about..."
- Free resource downloads (once we have lead magnets live)

**Review cadence:** monthly, not weekly. Social is a long game. Weekly metric-watching creates anxiety and bad decisions.

## FOUNDER VOICE

### What the founder is and isn't

The founder is not a spokesperson for the brand. The brand talks about problems, systems, and results.

The founder talks about how he thinks, what he's seen, what he's learned, what surprised him, and what he got wrong. He's a person with a perspective, not a company with a pitch.

Same values. Different angle. More personal.

### The one distinction

Brand posts: "Here's what happens in businesses like yours."

Founder posts: "Here's what I've seen. Here's what I think. Here's what changed my mind."

If a post could come from either, it belongs on the brand account, not the founder's.

---

### What the founder posts about

**1. What he sees in the field**
Observations from real work, research, and conversations. Anonymized, specific, honest. Not "businesses struggle with operations" but a specific pattern he noticed.

**2. How he thinks through a problem**
The reasoning process, not just the conclusion. Show the thinking, not just the answer.

**3. What he got wrong or changed his mind about**
Real position shifts, real mistakes, real recalibrations. This builds trust faster than any proof post.

**4. What he disagrees with**
A specific position in the market or industry that he thinks is wrong, and why. Specific and defensible.

**5. What building Autom8Labs has taught him**
Business building, delivery, team dynamics, what running this specific type of company has shown him that he didn't expect.

**6. AI insights grounded in experience**
Where AI actually helps. Where it doesn't. What most people get wrong about implementing AI. What should exist before you automate anything. This is credible territory right now because it comes from real research and thinking.

---

### What the founder avoids posting

- Motivational content without a specific situation behind it
- Vague "leadership lessons"
- Personal life unless directly connected to a business insight
- Celebrations of vanity metrics
- "Grateful and humbled" posts of any kind
- Opinions on AI trends without connection to a specific problem or observation
- Anything the brand account would post

---

### Founder tone

Brand voice: smart person over coffee. Clear. No personal texture.

Founder voice: same smart person, but you're hearing their personal take. Slightly more blunt. More willing to name something uncomfortable. More visible as a human with opinions and experience.

Still passes both filters, plus one additional:

**Filter 3:** Is this something only he could say, from his specific experience, observations, and perspective?

If anyone could say it, it goes to the brand account or it doesn't get posted.

---

### What the founder can say credibly now vs. later

**What he can say now:**

- "What I've seen in research and why it surprised me."
- "How I think through a business problem."
- "Why I built Autom8Labs. What I believed before and what I believe now."
- "What I got wrong in my assumptions before we started."
- "The pattern that shows up in every service business we've analyzed."
- "Why most automation projects fail before they start."
- "Where AI actually helps in business. And where it doesn't."
- "What should exist before you automate anything."
- "What I learned talking to [type of business owner] about how their business actually runs."

These are observations from real research and thinking. Honest and credible at this stage.

**What he can't say credibly yet:**

- "Third audit this month where..."
- "Every client we work with has..."
- "After implementing this system, we saw..."
- "The pattern across our clients is..."

These imply volume and experience that doesn't exist yet.

**The transition:** when real clients exist, the founder's voice expands immediately. Until then: think out loud, analyze publicly, build credibility through quality of thinking.

**The test:** "Could I back this up in a conversation if someone asked how I know this?" If yes, post it.

---

### How founder and brand amplify each other

They work together. They don't duplicate each other.

When the brand posts a Proof piece, the founder can post the thinking behind it. When the founder posts an observation, the brand can post the system that addresses it.

They never post the same thing in the same week. They never contradict each other.

**The rule:** if you can swap the author and the post still makes sense, rewrite it or reassign it.

**The one sentence:** The brand shows what we do. The founder shows why we do it and how we think.

## OPEN DECISIONS

Things deliberately left open. Resolved by real market feedback, not internal debate.

---

**1. Category positioning**

Current working frame: "AI partner" for businesses. Not locked in as a formal label. We're using it as a concept woven through communication, not stamped as a tagline.

The real answer comes from how clients describe us after working with us. After the first 2-3 real engagements, listen for how they introduce us to other people. That's the positioning.

---

**2. Pricing validation**

Current numbers are estimates based on market research and the service menu, not validated by real engagements.

The first 2-3 engagements will clarify what the market will actually pay. Until then, pricing stays flexible and per-engagement. All services priced in AUD for Australian clients, billed in USD via Autom8Lab LLC.

---

**3. Content system infrastructure**

The strategy in this doc is the "what." The infrastructure being built (Telegram bot for video intake, Claude API ideation engine, Notion content database, TokScript for transcript extraction) is the "how."

As the content system matures, manual processes described here will shift to system-assisted workflows. This doc should be updated as each phase goes live.
